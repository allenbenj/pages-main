Prosecutor Is Not Held To Any Judicial Standard

Analysis of Evidentiary Admission and Prosecutorial Compliance

This analysis examines the admissibility of a partial screenshot of electronic messages (State's Exhibit 4) in a North Carolina criminal trial, focusing on the governing rules of evidence, the prosecutor's adherence to the trial court's conditional directive, and the broader legal implications.

Drawing upon the North Carolina Rules of Evidence, codified in N.C. Gen. Stat. § 8C-1, and informed by pertinent case law, the issue is whether the prosecutor's conduct aligned with evidentiary standards and professional obligations. The controlling concerns are fairness, due process, and judicial integrity.

Governing North Carolina Rules

This issue is governed primarily by Rules 104, 901, 106, 801(d), 1003, 602, and 611(a), along with Rules 401 and 402 on relevance. If the prosecutor knowingly bypassed the court's condition or presented evidence with false attribution, Rules 3.3 and 3.4 of the North Carolina Rules of Professional Conduct are implicated as well.

Rule 104(b): Conditional Relevance

The judge's ruling exemplifies Rule 104(b), which permits admission of evidence only upon proof of a foundational fact on which relevance depends. Here, the screenshot was conditioned on testimony establishing a prior bonfire discussion before December 3, 2016, at 1:49 p.m.

That requirement was mandatory, not advisory. Failure to supply the linkage invited exclusion or striking.

Rule 901: Authentication

Rule 901 requires evidence sufficient to support a finding that the item is what the proponent claims it is. For electronic messages, that can come from witness knowledge, distinctive characteristics, or circumstantial proof.

Here, later messages identifying “Matt” as the user created a genuine authorship problem. Without contextual linkage, the foundation weakens materially.

Rule 106: Completeness

Rule 106 prevents selective introduction of only the favorable portion of a writing or statement. The judge recognized that the State offered only the first page while later pages suggested another user.

Presenting a partial exhibit without the exculpatory context risked misleading the jury and intensified the need for the condition the court imposed.

Rules 801(d), 1003, 602, and 611(a)

Even if the screenshot could qualify as a party-opponent admission or a duplicate, those rules do not excuse the need for attribution and proper sequencing. Rule 602 limits testimony to personal knowledge, and Rule 611(a) gives the court authority to control how proof is presented.

Did Bartholomew Follow the Judge's Instruction?

No. The judge twice required testimony linking the exhibit to a prior conversation before 1:49 p.m. on December 3, 2016, so the page would have context and a plausible basis for attribution.

Instead, the prosecutor elicited testimony about the exhibit's date, timestamp, sender, content, the witness's reply, a later bonfire photo, and general bonfire attendance. Generic bonfire references do not establish the specific prior discussion the court said was necessary.

The condition remained unfulfilled, undermining the Rule 104(b) framework and exposing the exhibit to exclusion, striking, or appellate challenge.

What This Means Legally

Evidentiary Consequence

Failure to satisfy Rule 104(b) makes the evidence vulnerable to exclusion and, if prejudicial, potentially reversible on appeal.

Fairness / Completeness Problem

A partial thread that omits authorship disclaimers risks misleading the jury and heightens the due process concern.

Authentication Problem

Without the promised linkage, attribution to the defendant weakens materially and may fail Rule 901's threshold.

Bottom Line

The judge imposed a clear, binding condition under Rule 104(b): proof of a prior discussion to contextualize and authenticate the screenshot. Bartholomew failed to elicit that proof in the testimony reflected here.

On this record, the admission was improper. If the non-compliance was intentional, it also implicates duties of candor and fairness to the tribunal. At minimum, the episode shows a breakdown in evidentiary rigor serious enough to threaten the legitimacy of the verdict. If this were the only issue with this case it would be a strong ground for a reversal if there had been a conviction. But it is just one of many, and the cumulative effect of all the issues is overwhelming.
