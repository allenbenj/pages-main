Judicial Duty and Courtroom Fairness

The courtroom is not supposed to be a passive stage where one side manufactures advantage while the other side absorbs the damage. A trial judge has an affirmative responsibility to manage the room, enforce fairness, and prevent the proceeding from becoming an instrument of pressure instead of a search for truth.

The question was not whether Happel would tell the truth. It was whether the State's case had been manufactured before the jury ever sat down.
— Core Issue, State v. Allen

In a case like State v. Benjamin Allen, that responsibility matters because the challenge is not confined to one disputed fact. The record raises questions about contradictions, charging decisions, evidentiary framing, and whether the process itself was allowed to drift away from neutrality. Once that happens, judicial management is no longer procedural housekeeping. It becomes the difference between a fair hearing and an institution ratifying its own failures.

The point of this section is to frame that responsibility clearly: to protect the jury from distortion, to restrain unfair prejudice, to force the parties back toward the actual record, and to preserve the legitimacy of the courtroom when the surrounding system appears unwilling to do so on its own.

MEMORANDUM

Core Responsibilities — And Where They Failed

The four responsibilities below define the minimum standard for judicial management of a criminal trial. In State v. Allen, each was tested — and each was left unmet.

Control The Proceeding

Keep argument, evidence, and witness presentation anchored to the record rather than theater, intimidation, or narrative drift. The court allowed a coached witness to testify without curative instruction and admitted unauthenticated exhibits over objection.

Protect Neutrality

Prevent one-sided advantage created by improper aggravation, unsupported insinuation, or selective treatment of contradictory evidence. The court permitted "illustrative" photos without foundation while the bar video — objective documentation — went unused.

Guard The Jury

Ensure the jury receives a fair record instead of a managed impression shaped by omission, confusion, or institutional credibility alone. The jury heard coached testimony and saw incomplete exhibits — then acquitted 12–0 on the primary charge.

Correct Process Failure

When the investigation or charging process shows signs of misconduct, the court has a duty to confront it rather than normalize it. The court recognized Bartholomew had "overstepped her bounds" — then did nothing.

The "Level Playing Field" Illusion

Judges are supposed to be neutral referees. The rules of evidence are supposed to apply equally to both sides. But in State v. Allen, the trial transcript reveals a consistent and disturbing asymmetry: when the prosecution stumbled, the judge bent over backward to help; when the defense sought to explore context, the judge nailed the law squarely and shut the door.

When the State's evidence is threatened by a technicality, the judge feels justified in relaxing the rules to "get to the truth." But when the Defense tries to explore an alternative truth, the judge suddenly becomes a strict constructionist, using the rules of evidence as a shield to protect the State's narrative from being challenged.
— The Level Playing Field Illusion

When the Prosecution Stumbles

  • The judge invents a non-hearsay exception to admit blatant hearsay without the prosecutor stating a purpose.
  • The judge uses an "illustrative purposes" workaround to admit photos lacking foundation.
  • The judge supplies the missing theory of authentication for Exhibit 4 when the State cannot establish authorship.
  • The standard is loose, accommodating, and focused on letting the emotional narrative proceed.
versus

When the Defense Seeks Context

  • The judge strictly enforces rules of form, sustaining objections to "broad" defense questions.
  • The defense is forced to be hyper-specific, technically perfect, and confined to a microscopic scope.
  • The standard is rigid, unforgiving, and focused on shutting down narrative exploration.
The prosecution gets to paint with a broad brush. The defense is forced to paint with a tiny one. This asymmetry creates a profound imbalance in how the story of the trial is told — and the jury sees it.
— The Structural Imbalance

The Practical Consequences

1

Prosecution Broad Brush

Emotional, third-party perceptions and loosely authenticated evidence build a cohesive, compelling narrative for the jury.

2

Defense Tiny Brush

Held to the absolute strictest letter of the law. Prevented from exploring context or alternative explanations unless perfectly framed.

3

Jury Perception Distorted

The defense looks evasive, nitpicky, or unable to offer a coherent alternative story — while the State's narrative flows unimpeded.

This is not merely a complaint about losing evidentiary rulings. It is a structural observation about how judicial bias operates in practice. The trial court consistently relaxed the rules of evidence to permit the State to introduce prejudicial, uncross-examined, and emotionally charged testimony, while simultaneously applying a hyper-strict, technically unforgiving standard to the Defense's attempts to elicit relevant contextual information. This asymmetrical application of the rules deprived the Defendant of a fair trial before a neutral arbiter and prevented the jury from hearing a complete and balanced presentation of the facts.

The Happel Colloquy: The Factual Record

Steven Happel was a State's witness — called by the prosecution, not the defense. Before trial, the prosecution attempted to pressure him not to appear. He ignored those threats and came anyway. On the morning of trial, before Happel was called to testify, defense counsel brought to the court's attention that Happel had reported an improper contact with the prosecution team. The court conducted an in-chambers colloquy, placed Happel under oath, and took the following sworn account.

In-Chambers Colloquy — Sworn Testimony of Steven Happel

State v. Benjamin Allen • Trial Morning • Under Oath
1 THE COURT Mr. Happel, you've been placed under oath. Please describe what you observed yesterday in the courthouse lobby.
2 HAPPEL I was told to present myself at 2 p.m. yesterday in the old courthouse, and I waited in the lobby. I did go out to my car to kind of relax. I came back in. I talked to the investigator Scott. I don't know his last name. I forgot. That's the only person I talked to about the case, answering questions.
3 HAPPEL And as I was sitting in the main lobby around 2 to 3 p.m. yesterday, Ms. Bartholomew and the victim and other witnesses, maybe, or her family walked in through the front door and were talking about the trial and how the defendant pleaded or was going to plead, something like that.
4 HAPPEL And then I heard Ms. Bartholomew tell the victim that she should have said that the victim was afraid of the witnesses, which is me and Joseph DeMarco.
5 HAPPEL And I felt intimidated, maybe like my feelings or thought process was trying to be played with, and felt very uncomfortable.
6 THE COURT Did you confront Ms. Bartholomew about what you heard?
7 HAPPEL No, sir. I did not.
8 THE COURT Do you believe this incident will affect your testimony here today?
9 HAPPEL The only answers I'll give are truthful answers that I can recall.
10 THE COURT I'm satisfied this witness is prepared to tell the truth, the whole truth and nothing but the truth, as he indicated under his oath. So we'll go ahead, then, bring in the jury at this time.
The court heard a sworn account of prosecutorial witness-coaching in the courthouse lobby — and its sole response was to ask the witness if he'd still tell the truth.
— The Framing Mistake

Happel further testified that he did not confront Bartholomew at the time, that he believed the incident would affect the trial, and that the ethical question "kind of disturbed" him. The court asked whether he believed the contact would affect his testimony. Happel replied: "The only answers I'll give are truthful answers that I can recall."

The jury was brought in. Happel testified for the State — without curative instruction, without investigation of the coaching allegation, and without sanction or admonishment of ADA Bartholomew.

The Hearsay Double Standard: When the Judge Became the Prosecutor's Advocate

The asymmetry in evidentiary rulings was not subtle. It was captured with precision in the trial transcript. One exchange in particular crystallizes the problem — the judge's handling of a hearsay objection that reveals exactly how the "level playing field" collapsed.

The Hearsay That Wasn't — Trial Testimony of Lauren Deal

Direct Examination • State v. Allen
1 LAUREN DEAL I feel like he was just as scared as I was. I mean, you know, in the car ride to the hospital, you know, he had said that he would not let anything else happen to me. He would not take me back over there. His girlfriend had been through something similar, and his exact words were, my girlfriend would kill me if I didn't help you.
2 MR. WUNSCH Objection, your honor.
3 THE COURT Well, clearly not being offered for the truth of the matter asserted, so I'll overrule the objection.
4 MS. BARTHOLOMEW [No argument offered. No purpose stated.]
The judge essentially acted as an advocate for the prosecution by supplying the legal justification out of thin air. The prosecutor did not make any argument or state a purpose for the evidence.
— Procedural Error Analysis

Why the Objection Was Valid — And Why the Ruling Failed

Textbook Hearsay

The witness testified about out-of-court statements made by a third party (the driver). Under N.C. R. Evid. 801, this is the textbook definition of hearsay. The judge overruled by claiming the statements were "clearly not being offered for the truth" — but the prosecutor never articulated any non-hearsay purpose.

"Effect on the Listener" Fails Here

A victim's subjective feeling of safety during a car ride to the hospital is generally irrelevant to whether the defendant committed the alleged assault. The statement "my girlfriend would kill me if I didn't help you" was offered to explain why the driver helped — but a bystander's motive for helping is entirely irrelevant to the defendant's guilt.

The "Trojan Horse" of Implicit Validation

By allowing the statement, the prosecution subtly used the driver as an unsworn character witness to validate the victim's story. The implicit message: "Even this bystander recognized how serious this was." This is classic bolstering — using a third party's untested perception to reinforce the narrative without calling that third party to testify.

Deprivation of Fair Opportunity to Respond

When a judge supplies the rationale for the prosecutor, it deprives the defense of a fair opportunity to object to that specific rationale. If the prosecutor had actually argued "effect on the listener," defense counsel could have immediately countered that the listener's state of mind was not a material issue. By jumping in, the judge short-circuited the adversarial process.

What Should Have Happened

The Procedurally Correct Sequence

How a Neutral Judge Would Have Handled the Objection
1 MR. WUNSCH Objection, your honor. Hearsay.
2 THE COURT Ms. Bartholomew, what is the non-hearsay purpose for which this statement is being offered? [The judge puts the burden back on the prosecutor.]
3 MS. BARTHOLOMEW [Either states a valid, relevant purpose, or remains silent.]
4 THE COURT Objection sustained. The jury will disregard the witness's last statement. [If no valid purpose is stated.]

By skipping the critical step — requiring the prosecutor to articulate a valid purpose — and inventing the rationale herself, the judge committed a clear error in evidentiary procedure. Worse, the judge should have struck the testimony and given a curative instruction, because the prejudicial statements were already in the record. No such instruction was ever given. The jury heard the driver's emotional validation and was left to weigh it as though it were properly admitted evidence.

The Court's Failure — And What Should Have Happened

The trial court's response can be summarized in three sentences: it held a colloquy, found Happel credible enough to testify, and brought the jury in. That is all. No investigation. No mistrial. No disqualification. No curative instruction. No referral to the State Bar.

The Framing Mistake

The court treated the incident as a credibility question — "Are you prepared to tell the truth?" — rather than an ethical crisis requiring structural intervention. This converted a prosecutorial misconduct problem into a witness reliability problem. The question was not whether Happel would tell the truth; it was whether the State's case had been manufactured before the jury ever sat down. The court's own language recognized that Bartholomew had "overstepped her bounds" — yet the remedy was nothing.

The alleged conduct — a prosecutor coaching the complaining witness to fabricate fear of the State's own witnesses — is among the most serious forms of prosecutorial misconduct. Four remedies were available and required:

A. Mistrial or Continuance

Where a prosecutor is alleged to have coached a witness, the contamination is not curable by a simple instruction — the testimony is tainted at its source. See N.C. Gen. Stat. § 15A-1061. A mistrial, or at minimum a continuance to permit investigation and potential substitution of counsel, was the constitutionally appropriate response.

B. Prosecutor Disqualification

North Carolina courts have authority to disqualify a prosecutor whose conduct violates the rules of professional conduct or deprives the defendant of a fair trial. See N.C. RPC 3.8, N.C. RPC 8.4. Directing a witness to fabricate an emotional response is precisely the conduct requiring disqualification. No step was taken.

C. Curative Instruction

Even without a mistrial or disqualification, the court was obligated to instruct the jury that an attorney's attempt to influence witness testimony is improper, and that testimony aligning with litigation strategy rather than independent recollection should be scrutinized. No such instruction was given.

D. Referral to Oversight Authorities

The court was obligated to report the misconduct to the North Carolina State Bar. See N.C. RPC 3.3, N.C. RPC 8.3. The State Bar had already received complaints about Bartholomew and taken no action. The court's failure to report ensured the Bar's file remained incomplete — and its inaction insulated.

The Pattern Beyond Happel

The coaching incident was not isolated. It was one thread in a fabric of evidentiary manipulation spanning the entire prosecution.

State's Exhibit 4 — The Authentication Failure

December 3, 2016 Facebook Messages • Conditional Admission • Rule 104(b)
1 MS. BARTHOLOMEW Your Honor, at this time, the State would introduce State's Exhibit No. 4.
2 MR. WUNSCH Objection. In this message, it clearly states — this is not Ben. It's Matt. I'm using his phone. And the last message states later that Ben says, I wasn't even — I didn't even have possession of my phone. Matt had my phone. So it's not Ben. It's clearly in the text; it's not Ben. We're picking out one page.
3 THE COURT [After off-record bench discussion and jury removal] If she testifies that they had a discussion prior to December 3, 2016, at 1:49, then that would give this particular page context. They will need to link this up with a conversation prior to December 3, 2016.
4 THE COURT [After jury returned] Ladies and gentlemen, in my discretion, I have overruled the objection, found the proposed exhibit number four to be admissible and sufficiently reliable to be admitted.
The judge supplied the missing theory of authentication for the prosecution. The condition was never satisfied — but the evidence remained before the jury.
— Exhibit 4 Analysis

The Authentication Problem

The exhibit's own content identified the sender as "Matt" using the defendant's phone. The defendant himself stated he did not have possession of his phone. Under N.C. R. Evid. 901, authentication is a condition precedent to admissibility. The displayed account name does not automatically identify the person operating the device — especially when the State's own exhibit shows that control of the account was disputed.

The Judge Supplied the Missing Theory

Instead of requiring the prosecution to resolve the defect through competent evidence, the judge developed a conditional theory: if the witness testified to a prior bonfire conversation before 1:49 p.m., the message could be connected to Allen. The judge did not merely evaluate evidence — the judge identified the exact additional fact that could salvage the State's deficient foundation.

The Condition Was Never Satisfied

The ADA asked about bonfire history ("maybe five") but never asked whether the witness and Allen had discussed that particular bonfire before 1:49 p.m. on December 3, 2016. A history of attending bonfires does not establish the specific prior conversation on which the court based its authentication ruling. The condition remained unsatisfied — yet the exhibit stayed in evidence.

The ADA Exceeded the Court's Limitation

The judge stated the State was "simply trying to introduce the very first one." But the ADA elicited the witness's response, its timestamp, a further statement attributed to Allen, a photograph of a bonfire, and another response — expanding the evidence beyond the single message used to justify the ruling. The court allowed it without correction.

The judge told the jury the exhibit was "sufficiently reliable." That was not a neutral evidentiary ruling. It was an endorsement of the State's disputed evidence — addressing the very factual issue the defense disputed.
— Judicial Endorsement of Disputed Evidence

The Rule of Completeness Violation

The State offered only the first page of the Facebook exchange. Later pages identified another user of the phone and undermined the attribution the State was attempting to prove. Under N.C. R. Evid. 106, the defense was entitled to have the complete context admitted contemporaneously — not as a separate defense exhibit offered later. The judge recognized this problem yet placed the burden of completeness on the defense, allowing the State to create the initial narrative and requiring the defense to undo it after the jury had already heard the prosecution's version.

Why This Matters: The Cumulative Harm

The prejudice from Exhibit 4 was not confined to the words in the messages. The ruling affected the jury's perception of the entire trial. The jurors saw the defense raise a specific, fact-based objection; the judge reject the defense's position; the judge describe the State's evidence as reliable; the prosecutor proceed beyond the stated limitation; and no judicial correction when the required foundation failed to materialize. The implicit message was powerful: the State's evidence was trustworthy, the defense's objections lacked merit, and the prosecutor had the court's approval.

DeMarco's Distorted Statement

Freeman's affidavit claimed DeMarco "made the decision to get his vehicle and ride in an attempt to locate her." DeMarco's recorded statement says he got afraid and left — he incidentally saw Deal while driving. Freeman upgraded a chance encounter into a deliberate search. Deal said she hid behind a tree; DeMarco said she ran through a field. Both cannot be true. The affidavit was used for probable cause and never corrected.

Illustrative Evidence Without Foundation

The State offered photos of the woods and residence during Deal's direct examination. The court overruled foundation objections, admitted them "for illustrative purposes," and permitted Deal to step down from the stand to point at locations — a performative walkthrough under conditions she claimed were complete darkness and unfamiliar terrain. The bar video, which would have provided objective documentation, went unused.

The Cumulative Effect

Each ruling was framed in terms of discretion — "illustrative purposes," "sufficiently reliable" — but the cumulative effect was to lower the State's evidentiary burden at precisely the points where the record was weakest. The court did not merely err in individual rulings; it created a procedural environment in which the State could present a manufactured case with minimal judicial scrutiny.

The "Misunderstanding": Plea Negotiation Misrepresentation

During plea discussions, ADA Bartholomew represented to the defendant: "We found your sperm and DNA on her." This representation was false. The SBI report, dated May 11, 2020, stated: "No spermatozoa present." The Y-STR analysis found a non-individualizing profile matching 1 in 518 Caucasian males — including all paternal relatives — a result that could not be attributed to the defendant with any specificity.

A prosecutor with ten years of sexual assault experience, a district court judgeship, Secret Service training, and UNC certification in evidence interpretation does not "misunderstand" whether sperm evidence exists.

The Consequences: What the Coaching Produced

The Linguistic Shift

Forensic linguistic analysis comparing Deal's 2019 statements to her 2023 trial testimony reveals cosine similarity collapsing from 1.0 to 0.28. The shift is directional:

  • She began claiming she "froze" — a term absent from her 2019 interviews.
  • She began invoking "fear of witnesses" — precisely what Bartholomew was heard coaching.
  • Her vocabulary shifted from concrete ("truck," "bathroom," "keys") to legalistic ("force," "fear," "trust," "freezing," "triggers").

This is not natural memory evolution. It is the signature of coached testimony: the witness moves from "what happened" to "what the jury needs to hear."

The Jury's Answer

The jury acquitted Benjamin Allen of first-degree forcible rape 12-0 — a unanimous rejection of the charge most dependent on the coached narrative. The hung jury on the lesser charge does not validate the prosecution; it reflects the impossibility of achieving unanimity when the evidence is compromised and the witness has been coached. A prosecutor who manufactures testimony creates a fragile foundation that cannot sustain a unanimous conviction on the primary charge.

Bench Book Reference: What the Judge Should Have Consulted

A trial judge confronted with alleged prosecutorial witness-coaching does not need to invent the response. The authorities below define the minimum standard. None were followed.

North Carolina Judicial Bench Book — Applicable Authorities

N.C. Gen. Stat. § 15A-1061

Mistrial authority. Where prosecutor is alleged to have coached a witness, contamination is not curable by simple instruction — testimony is tainted at its source.

N.C. RPC 3.8

Special responsibilities of a prosecutor. The prosecutor is a minister of justice whose duty is to seek justice, not merely to obtain or preserve a conviction.

N.C. RPC 8.4

Professional misconduct. Directing a witness to fabricate an emotional response to defense witnesses is precisely the type of conduct requiring disqualification and discipline.

N.C. RPC 3.3 & 8.3

Candor toward tribunal and reporting professional misconduct. The court was obligated to report the misconduct to the North Carolina State Bar and judicial oversight authorities.

N.C. R. Evid. 901

Authentication requirement. The proponent must produce evidence sufficient to support a finding that the item is what the proponent claims it is. A displayed account name does not automatically identify the person operating the device.

N.C. R. Evid. 104(b)

Conditional relevance. Evidence admitted subject to later introduction of connecting fact — but the required connecting evidence must actually be introduced. The rule does not authorize retention of the benefit after failing to establish the condition.

N.C. R. Evid. 106

Rule of Completeness. When a writing or recorded statement is introduced, the adverse party may require introduction of any other part that in fairness ought to be considered at the same time.

N.C. R. Evid. 403

Exclusion of relevant evidence on grounds of prejudice, confusion, or waste of time. Even marginally relevant evidence must be excluded if probative value is substantially outweighed by danger of unfair prejudice.

N.C. Gen. Stat. § 7A-49.4

Superior-court criminal calendaring. The district attorney calendars cases, proposes trial dates, publishes the trial calendar, announces the intended order of call, and consults on new dates for cases not reached — the statutory opportunity structure for strategic calendaring analysis.

N.C. Code of Judicial Conduct

A judge should disqualify when impartiality may reasonably be questioned, and should initiate appropriate disciplinary measures for a lawyer’s unprofessional conduct when the judge becomes aware of it.

The court did not hold the State to the authentication condition it imposed. It did not hold the ADA to the scope it announced. It did not ensure that the account-sharing context accompanied the State's selected evidence. It did not refrain from endorsing the reliability of disputed evidence before the jury. At each decision point, the benefit went to the prosecution.
— Summary of Judicial Failures

Institutional Familiarity, Strategic Calendaring & Asymmetric Trial Management

The record surrounding the prosecution of Benjamin Scott Allen supports a legitimate inquiry into whether Assistant District Attorney Tiffany Bartholomew used the prosecution’s calendaring influence to obtain a trial setting before Judge Paul A. Holcombe because she expected favorable discretionary treatment.

Motive Opportunity Apparent Benefit Not Yet Conclusive Proof
The strongest defensible conclusion is not that judge shopping has already been conclusively proved. It is that the record supplies evidence of motive, opportunity, and benefit, making strategic calendaring a reasonable theory that should be tested against the complete calendar, continuance, assignment, and communication records.
— Framing the Inquiry

The existing evidence does not conclusively prove an agreement between Bartholomew and Holcombe, an improper personal relationship, or the intentional selection of Holcombe by name. It does, however, establish or support the following:

Institutional Setting

  • Bartholomew and Holcombe were experienced participants in the same regional criminal-justice system by the time of Allen’s trial.
  • North Carolina law gave the District Attorney’s Office substantial control over the scheduling and ordering of superior-court criminal cases.
  • The Allen prosecution was continued repeatedly over several years, creating multiple opportunities for strategic selection of the week or judicial session in which the case would finally be tried.

What Happened at Trial

  • Holcombe repeatedly resolved disputed issues in ways that preserved the State’s case and minimized the consequences of prosecutorial irregularities.
  • When a State witness testified under oath that he heard Bartholomew apparently coach the complaining witness to add a claim of fear, Holcombe did not investigate whether Bartholomew had attempted to alter the complaining witness’s account.
  • Instead, Holcombe reframed the issue as whether the reporting witness, Steven Happel, could still testify truthfully and resumed the trial immediately.

Disclosure & Record Gaps

  • Critical medical records were reportedly produced only on the day of trial, yet the prosecution was permitted to proceed.
  • Other potentially favorable or impeaching information—including the relationship between Detective Steve Freeman and State witness Carmen Love—apparently never reached the defense record.

Asymmetry Already Documented

The trial court’s evidentiary rulings displayed a recurring asymmetry: flexibility when the prosecution encountered evidentiary problems and rigid technical enforcement when the defense sought broader context. That pattern is developed above; it is the benefit half of the calendaring inquiry.

What the JCPC Structure Actually Establishes

Statutory Membership

County JCPC membership should include the district attorney or designee, and the chief district court judge or a designated judge — plus law-enforcement, juvenile-court, social-services, mental-health, education, business, nonprofit, and community representatives.

What Councils Do

JCPCs assess local juvenile needs, identify service gaps, help allocate funding, evaluate programs, and seek appropriate treatment and intermediate dispositional options.

What Structure Alone Does Not Prove

Without council rosters, minutes, attendance records, committee assignments, or grant documents, the structure does not establish that Holcombe and Bartholomew personally worked together on Teen Court, restitution, Project Catch, counseling, detention screening, or any other identified program.

Accurate Narrow Point

They were not strangers in entirely separate professional worlds. Both had long careers in prosecution and in juvenile or special-victim matters, and each held institutional roles that often required interaction among judges, prosecutors, law enforcement, court counselors, and community programs. The precise nature and duration of their direct professional relationship must be established through records rather than assumed.

The Relevant Professional Relationship

The meaningful relationship for purposes of Allen’s trial is not necessarily a private friendship or shared JCPC membership. It is the combination of institutional familiarity; repeated professional contact; prosecutorial reliance on a judge’s discretionary rulings; the District Attorney’s Office’s calendaring authority; and a history of courtroom decisions that consistently permitted the State to preserve its preferred narrative.

Prior professional familiarity between a judge and prosecutor is common and, standing alone, does not establish bias. A court system could not operate if every judge were disqualified whenever the judge had previously worked with an attorney appearing before the court. The significance changes when familiarity is combined with evidence that:

1

Calendaring Leverage

The prosecutor had some ability to influence when the case would be tried, and the case was repeatedly continued until a particular judge was presiding.

2

One-Way Latitude

The judge repeatedly excused or minimized irregularities benefiting the prosecutor, while similar latitude was not extended to the defense.

3

Containment

Serious allegations concerning the prosecutor’s own conduct were contained rather than investigated.

The question is not whether Holcombe and Bartholomew merely knew one another. The question is whether their professional familiarity contributed to a courtroom dynamic in which Bartholomew’s assertions were accorded presumptive trust, while defense objections were met with skepticism.
— The Real Inquiry

Under the North Carolina Code of Judicial Conduct, a judge should disqualify himself or herself when the judge’s impartiality may reasonably be questioned. The Code also instructs judges to initiate appropriate disciplinary measures for a lawyer’s unprofessional conduct when the judge becomes aware of it.

Happel Reframed: What the Court Chose Not to Ask

The Happel incident is developed in full above. For the calendaring analysis, its significance is narrower and sharper: it placed a sworn allegation of apparent prosecutorial coaching directly before the court — and the court converted that allegation into a limited inquiry about whether the reporting witness remained available.

Happel Was the State’s Next Witness

The transcript begins with the court asking who the State’s next witness was, and Bartholomew answering: “Steven Happel, Your Honor.” He was not formally identified at that moment as a defense witness. Bartholomew called him the State’s next witness, apparently out of order to accommodate his schedule.

Calling him as a State witness does not eliminate the possibility that the prosecution considered him dangerous or uncooperative. A prosecutor may call a known eyewitness because the witness cannot be omitted credibly, because the defense will call him anyway, or because presenting him first allows the State to control the initial examination.

Irregular Subpoena Circumstances

Happel lived in Huntsville, Alabama, and worked as an FBI contractor. He testified that he received an emailed subpoena on the Thursday before he was expected to appear the following Monday, and that the subpoena contained an alias or incorrect version of his name.

When asked how he managed to attend on such short notice: “I told my work that I didn’t have a choice and I didn’t want to risk my clearance or getting in trouble.” That statement shows perceived legal and professional pressure — and raises questions about why a known eyewitness in a years-old prosecution was handled as an emergency immediately before testimony.

The Wording That Matters

Happel testified under oath that, while waiting in the courthouse lobby, he overheard Bartholomew speaking with Deal and others associated with her:

“I heard Ms. Bartholomew tell the victim that she should have said that the victim was afraid of the witnesses, which is me and Joseph DeMarco.”
— Steven Happel, Under Oath

Happel did not report hearing Bartholomew ask whether Deal had been afraid, whether Deal had omitted something, why Deal had acted in a particular manner, or whether Deal wanted to clarify a prior answer. He reported a directive concerning what Deal “should have said.” That wording reasonably supports an inference that Bartholomew identified an answer she believed would strengthen the State’s account and told Deal that she should have supplied it.

Procedural Containment

Holcombe concluded: “I’m satisfied this witness is prepared to tell the truth, the whole truth and nothing but the truth.” That conclusion addressed Happel’s reliability. It did not address the alleged conduct of Bartholomew or the possible alteration of Deal’s account.

The reasonably expected questions — Did Bartholomew make the statement? What did she mean? Had Deal previously claimed to fear Happel or DeMarco? Was Bartholomew attempting to supply a new explanation? Did she intend to elicit the new “fear” narrative? Were other witnesses present? Did surveillance preserve the conversation? — were never asked or answered.

The court ensured that the State’s witness remained available and that the trial could continue, while leaving the allegation against the prosecutor unresolved.

Connection to DeMarco

The alleged treatment of Happel also supplies a framework for examining DeMarco’s evolving account. The theory is not merely that two witnesses remembered events differently. It is that the State may have applied different pressures depending on whether a witness accepted the State’s narrative:

  • Happel allegedly resisted being told that Allen was guilty.
  • Happel later overheard the prosecutor suggesting that Deal should characterize him as someone she feared.
  • DeMarco’s account allegedly remained somewhat consistent with Deal’s account but shifted sufficiently toward the prosecution’s theory to obtain Freeman’s approval rather than suspicion or prosecution.
  • Freeman praised DeMarco in his reports once DeMarco accepted enough of the proposed narrative, changing a self-reported fear-based exit into a noble search-and-rescue event.

The chronology would permit an inference of a pressure-and-reward system: resistance produced suspicion, intimidation, or attempted discrediting; partial accommodation produced approval and protection.

The Larger Pattern Feeding the Calendaring Theory

The Happel event becomes more probative when considered with the treatment of other disputed matters already developed on this page — Exhibit 4, hearsay latitude, illustrative photos, and the plea misrepresentation. Several additional strands complete the pattern.

Medical Records on the Day of Trial

Critical medical records were not provided to the defense until the day of trial and were reportedly located in another judge’s box. Same-day production deprived the defense of a meaningful opportunity to review the records with an expert, compare them with the State’s prior representations, investigate inconsistencies, prepare cross-examination, determine whether additional records existed, revise trial strategy, and evaluate the accuracy of the prosecution’s assertions concerning injury or forensic evidence.

A disclosure is not meaningfully timely merely because it occurs before the evidence is formally introduced. N.C. RPC 3.8 requires a prosecutor, after reasonably diligent inquiry, to make timely disclosure of evidence required by law. The judicial issue includes what Holcombe did after learning of the lateness, whether he identified the cause, and whether the defense received an effective remedy.

Comparison: The Abraham Prosecution

In the later prosecution of Daniel Abraham, defense attorney William Pruden alleged that Bartholomew withheld video evidence that “exonerates the Defendant” until the day of trial and produced it only after being compelled. Abraham was acquitted of the felony charge, and the misdemeanor charge was dismissed.

The Abraham allegation does not, by itself, prove intentional misconduct in Allen. It is relevant because it presents a substantially similar disclosure structure involving the same prosecutor: materially favorable evidence; prolonged State possession or control; production at trial; judicial compulsion; and no publicly identifiable accountability directed at the prosecutor. The recurrence makes it less satisfactory to dismiss the Allen disclosure as an isolated administrative mistake.

Freeman–Carmen Love Relationship

The defense apparently was not informed that Detective Freeman had a family relationship with State witness Carmen Love — material to Freeman’s assessment of her credibility, the rigor of his interviews, informal communications, possible bias or loyalty, decisions to omit or reconcile portions of her account, and the independence of the investigation.

Freeman testified that his conversation with Carmen Love was part of his presentation to the magistrate even though her statements contradicted many critical elements of Lauren Deal’s law-enforcement interview. Carmen did not appear at trial once she realized there was video that proved she was not telling the truth. The omission supports one of three inferences: Freeman failed to disclose the relationship; Bartholomew knew and failed to disclose it; or Bartholomew failed to conduct a reasonably diligent inquiry. None reflects a fully transparent prosecution.

False Plea Premise Treated as Nonprejudicial

Misconduct by Bartholomew was acknowledged before Holcombe in chambers: a plea offer had been based on false information. Holcombe found no prejudice because the defense was given a DNA expert — an expert who was unable to testify because the prosecution overshot the time, requiring the expert to return home to another state for scheduled medical treatment.

That resembles the Happel inquiry: Happel still said he would testify truthfully, so the trial continued; Allen did not accept the allegedly false plea offer, so the court found no prejudice. In each instance, the court focused on whether the misconduct successfully achieved its apparent objective rather than whether the conduct itself threatened the integrity of the process.

Opportunity: Strategic Calendaring Under G.S. 7A-49.4

North Carolina law gives district attorneys substantial authority over superior-court criminal calendars. Under G.S. 7A-49.4:

District Attorney Calendar Authority

Calendaring

Criminal cases are calendared by the district attorney; the district attorney proposes trial dates and publishes the trial calendar.

Order of Trial

The calendar lists cases in the anticipated order of trial; the district attorney announces the intended order for calling cases.

Multiple Sessions

Separate calendars may be issued when multiple sessions are operating.

Cases Not Reached

The district attorney consults with the parties concerning new dates for cases that are not reached.

The presiding judge retains authority over deviations, continuances, and the actual call of cases. Thus, the prosecutor cannot simply appoint a judge. But the prosecutor may have the practical ability to influence:

Practical Influence Levers

  • the session in which a case is listed;
  • whether the State announces ready;
  • the order in which cases are called;
  • whether another case consumes the session;
  • the date proposed after a continuance;
  • which concurrent calendar receives the case; and
  • whether the case is reached while a known judge is presiding.

Why Continuances Matter

This is the mechanism through which indirect judge shopping could occur. The large number of continuances in Allen’s case matters because every reset potentially created another selection point.

The total number alone is not proof. The necessary analysis is whether the State’s conduct varied depending on the assigned judge.

Motive and Expected Benefit Under Holcombe

A viable judge-shopping theory requires more than opportunity. It requires a plausible reason to prefer a particular judge. The existing trial record identifies several potential benefits Bartholomew received from having Holcombe preside:

Obstacles Resolved for the State

  • Critical medical records were located in the judge’s inbox on the day of trial without terminating the prosecution.
  • A disputed partial message exhibit could be admitted through a judicially constructed authentication and completeness rationale.
  • The State could present broad contextual and emotional evidence with no event- or fact-driven testimony, while defense questioning was more narrowly confined.
  • The undisclosed Freeman–Love relationship did not generate a trial-level inquiry — and is still not disclosed even after the trial.
  • A false factual premise in plea negotiations could be treated as nonprejudicial because the defense was assigned an expert.
  • A threat could be made and followed through on if the plea wasn’t accepted.

Allegations Contained

  • A sworn report of apparent witness coaching was reduced to whether Happel remained capable of testifying.
  • Bartholomew was not questioned under oath or otherwise required to explain her reported statement to Deal.
  • The complaining witness was not questioned about whether she had been supplied with a new “fear” narrative.
  • The trial resumed immediately, preserving the State’s case and avoiding interruption, recusal, replacement of the prosecutor, or disciplinary referral.
The benefit was not necessarily that Holcombe would guarantee a conviction. The jury ultimately did not return the complete result sought by the State. The benefit was that procedural and evidentiary obstacles were repeatedly resolved, allowing the prosecution to continue presenting its theory without any factual basis or foundation in reality.
— What “Benefit” Means Here

The Judge-Shopping Inference

The analysis must distinguish what the record supports from what it has not yet established.

Facts Supporting the Inference

  • an unusually prolonged prosecution;
  • repeated continuances;
  • prosecutorial authority over the trial calendar;
  • knowledge of judicial assignments;
  • a judge with whom the prosecutor had established professional familiarity;
  • repeated favorable discretionary rulings;
  • minimal consequences for late disclosure;
  • containment of allegations concerning the prosecutor herself; and
  • differing levels of scrutiny applied to prosecution and defense conduct.
vs

Facts Not Yet Established

  • Bartholomew requested Holcombe by name;
  • Bartholomew caused a continuance because a different judge was assigned;
  • she announced ready only when Holcombe was scheduled;
  • she transferred the case between sessions to reach Holcombe;
  • she communicated privately with Holcombe concerning assignment of the case;
  • Holcombe agreed to accept the case for Bartholomew;
  • they jointly administered the same JCPC programs; or
  • Holcombe made rulings because of loyalty to Bartholomew rather than his understanding of the law.
The record presents evidence supporting an inference of strategic calendaring or apparent judge shopping, but calendar and assignment records are required to determine whether the prosecutor actually manipulated the case’s setting to obtain Judge Holcombe.
— Most Accurate Characterization

The professional relationship is relevant primarily as evidence of familiarity and expected treatment, not as independent proof of collusion. Those distinctions are recognized and made explicit here.

Alternative Explanations

A credible analysis must address the principal counterarguments.

1. Ordinary professional familiarity

Judges and prosecutors in the same region routinely know and work with one another. Familiarity does not establish bias.

Response Correct. The argument depends on the cumulative pattern of calendaring opportunity, favorable discretionary rulings, and failure to investigate prosecutorial conduct — not familiarity alone.

2. Legitimate continuances

Some continuances may have resulted from defense requests, unavailable witnesses, pandemic disruption, counsel changes, congested calendars, or case complexity.

Response Each continuance must be attributed accurately. Judge shopping cannot be inferred from continuances requested by the defense or required by circumstances outside the prosecutor’s control. The ADA did not provide discovery on time, forcing the defense to request a continuation until it could be provided.

3. Broad evidentiary discretion

Trial judges receive substantial deference in authentication, completeness, witness examination, and trial-management decisions.

Response The issue is cumulative asymmetry. Even individually permissible rulings can create a fairness concern when judicial discretion repeatedly cures State deficiencies while strictly limiting the defense.

4. Happel was allowed to testify

Holcombe did not prevent Happel from testifying. He placed Happel under oath, made a record, and confirmed that Happel would testify truthfully.

Response That addresses witness availability but not the reported coaching of Deal. Permitting Happel to testify did not resolve whether Bartholomew had attempted to change another witness’s account.

5. Bartholomew called Happel herself

Because Happel was identified as the State’s next witness, the transcript may contradict the proposition that Bartholomew sought to suppress him entirely.

Response The transcript does weaken any categorical claim that she refused to call him. It does not resolve whether she called him reluctantly, attempted to control or pressure him, or tried to discredit him after realizing that his account would not support the State. It actually supports the attempt to discredit him immediately once Deal found out that he went to sleep at the Defendant’s house that evening.

The following records would materially strengthen or defeat the judge-shopping inference:

Calendaring and Assignments

  • every weekly judicial assignment;
  • every published trial calendar listing Allen;
  • the order in which Allen was listed;
  • records of concurrent sessions;
  • dates when Bartholomew announced ready or not ready;
  • dates when other prosecutors controlled the case;

  • calendar-call recordings or minutes; and
  • the local criminal case docketing plan.

Continuances

  • every continuance motion;
  • the requesting party;
  • the stated reason;
  • the ruling judge;
  • any objection;
  • the next proposed date;
  • whether the assigned judge changed; and
  • whether the State’s readiness position changed with the assignment.

Communications

  • emails among the District Attorney’s Office, clerk, trial court administrator, and judicial staff;
  • communications concerning assignment to a particular session;

  • meeting minutes;

  • correspondence naming Holcombe or Bartholomew.

Those records would determine whether there was any direct institutional collaboration beyond their general professional roles.

Final Assessment: Motive, Opportunity, Benefit

The negative impact on the defense is cumulative. No single continuance proves judge shopping. No single evidentiary ruling proves bias. No prior professional contact proves collusion. No single late disclosure conclusively proves intentional suppression.

But the law does not require the public to view every incident in isolation. The Allen record presents a repeated directional pattern:

The Directional Pattern

Critical information arrives late; undisclosed relationships remain outside the defense record; disputed prosecution evidence is admitted through flexible reasoning; defense questioning is narrowly controlled; plea negotiations were based on inaccurate information and accompanied by a threat that was followed through on; an eyewitness reports pressure from the prosecutor and investigator; the same eyewitness hears the prosecutor tell the complaining witness what she “should have said”; the judge investigates whether the eyewitness will remain cooperative and truthful, but not whether the prosecutor attempted to alter the complaining witness’s story; and the prosecution continues without a meaningful institutional response from either the court or the North Carolina Bar.

1

Motive

That pattern supplies a rational motive for Bartholomew to prefer Holcombe’s courtroom.

2

Opportunity

North Carolina’s calendaring structure supplied the opportunity.

3

Apparent Benefit

Holcombe’s rulings and trial-management decisions supplied the apparent benefit.

What remains missing is evidence connecting those three elements to a deliberate calendaring decision.

What the Record Does — and Does Not — Establish

The available evidence does not yet conclusively prove that Tiffany Bartholomew selected Paul Holcombe to preside over the Allen trial. It does establish a nonfrivolous basis to investigate strategic calendaring. Bartholomew operated within a system that gave the District Attorney’s Office substantial control over trial settings; the case was repeatedly continued; and Holcombe subsequently managed significant prosecution irregularities in ways that preserved the State’s case.

Most notably, when a State witness testified under oath that Bartholomew told the complaining witness what she “should have said,” Holcombe did not investigate the prosecutor or the alleged coaching. He determined only that the reporting witness remained willing to testify truthfully and immediately continued the trial. Considered alongside same-day medical-record disclosure, disputed evidentiary rulings, the undisclosed Freeman–Love relationship, and the court’s asymmetrical treatment of prosecution and defense deficiencies, the circumstances reasonably raise the question whether Bartholomew maneuvered the case toward a judicial forum in which she expected accommodation rather than accountability.

The central inquiry is not whether Holcombe and Bartholomew once served together on a particular juvenile council. The current public record does not establish that. The central inquiry is: Who controlled each decision that ultimately placed the Allen case before Holcombe, what other judges were assigned when the case was continued or not reached, and did Bartholomew’s readiness and calendaring decisions change depending on who was scheduled to preside?
— Until the Records Are Produced

Until those records are produced, judge shopping remains an unresolved but evidence-supported inference — not a baseless accusation and not yet a conclusively proved fact.

What Relief Is Required

1. State Bar Referral

Formal investigation of ADA Bartholomew under N.C. RPC 3.3, 3.4, 3.8, and 8.4 for the Happel coaching, Exhibit 4 authentication failure, DeMarco statement distortion, and plea negotiation misrepresentation.

2. Judicial Standards Commission

Referral of the trial judge for failure to report known prosecutorial misconduct, overruling authentication and hearsay objections without adequate foundation, and failure to take remedial action when misconduct was brought to the court's attention under oath.

3. Case Review

Review of all cases prosecuted by Bartholomew, with attention to witness preparation, lobby conversations with witnesses, late-stage narrative shifts, conditional admission of electronic evidence, and plea negotiations involving forensic misrepresentations.

4. Record Preservation & Transparency

Preserve the Happel colloquy transcript, linguistic analysis, Exhibit 4 foundation hearings, and DeMarco recordings as part of the official record. The Lee County Clerk and AOC should publish a complete index of all complaints, investigations, and disciplinary actions related to this case.

5. Calendaring & Assignment Disclosure

Produce the complete superior-court master calendars, weekly judicial assignments, published trial calendars listing Allen, continuance motions with requesting party and stated reason, State readiness announcements, and communications concerning session assignment. Without those records, the strategic-calendaring inference cannot be confirmed or defeated.

6. Relationship & JCPC Records

Produce Johnston, Harnett, Lee, and Moore JCPC rosters, minutes, attendance, committee assignments, and conflict disclosures naming Holcombe or Bartholomew — not to assume collaboration, but to establish or eliminate any direct institutional collaboration beyond general professional roles.

Epilogue

The Happel incident is not a story about what happened during a trial. It is a story about what the system permitted to happen and then chose to ignore. A witness came forward under oath. He reported that the prosecutor had tried to shape his testimony and the complainant's testimony. The court heard him, thanked him, and sent him to the stand. The State Bar was introduced to the prosecutor's file and did nothing. The system worked exactly as designed: it protected the process from disruption, and it protected the prosecutor from accountability.

The defendant was acquitted of the primary charge — a testament to the jury's ability to see through manufactured testimony. But acquittal is not justice. Justice requires a clean process, uncoached witnesses, properly authenticated evidence, honest prosecutors, and officers of the court held to the standards they impose on everyone else. None of those things happened here.

The Happel incident remains in the record as a documented coaching attempt with no consequence. Exhibit 4 was admitted without the State satisfying the court's own condition. DeMarco's statement was distorted. The ADA lied about sperm evidence and called it a misunderstanding. Critical medical records arrived on the day of trial. An undisclosed investigator–witness relationship never reached the defense record. And the question whether the case was maneuvered toward a judicial forum that would accommodate those irregularities — rather than investigate them — remains unanswered only because the calendaring and assignment records have not been produced.

Judge shopping is not yet a conclusively proved fact. It is also not a baseless accusation. Motive, opportunity, and apparent benefit are already in the record. What is missing is the paper trail that would connect them to a deliberate calendaring decision.

It left the defendant to wonder whether justice is anything more than a word we use to describe the outcome when the machinery of the state decides to stop grinding.
