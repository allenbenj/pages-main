Judicial Duty and Courtroom Fairness

The courtroom is not supposed to be a passive stage where one side manufactures advantage while the other side absorbs the damage. A trial judge has an affirmative responsibility to manage the room, enforce fairness, and prevent the proceeding from becoming an instrument of pressure instead of a search for truth.

The question was not whether Happel would tell the truth. It was whether the State's case had been manufactured before the jury ever sat down.
— Core Issue, State v. Allen

In a case like State v. Benjamin Allen, that responsibility matters because the challenge is not confined to one disputed fact. The record raises questions about contradictions, charging decisions, evidentiary framing, and whether the process itself was allowed to drift away from neutrality. Once that happens, judicial management is no longer procedural housekeeping. It becomes the difference between a fair hearing and an institution ratifying its own failures.

The point of this section is to frame that responsibility clearly: to protect the jury from distortion, to restrain unfair prejudice, to force the parties back toward the actual record, and to preserve the legitimacy of the courtroom when the surrounding system appears unwilling to do so on its own.

Core Responsibilities — And Where They Failed

The four responsibilities below define the minimum standard for judicial management of a criminal trial. In State v. Allen, each was tested — and each was left unmet.

Control The Proceeding

Keep argument, evidence, and witness presentation anchored to the record rather than theater, intimidation, or narrative drift. The court allowed a coached witness to testify without curative instruction and admitted unauthenticated exhibits over objection.

Protect Neutrality

Prevent one-sided advantage created by improper aggravation, unsupported insinuation, or selective treatment of contradictory evidence. The court permitted "illustrative" photos without foundation while the bar video — objective documentation — went unused.

Guard The Jury

Ensure the jury receives a fair record instead of a managed impression shaped by omission, confusion, or institutional credibility alone. The jury heard coached testimony and saw incomplete exhibits — then acquitted 12–0 on the primary charge.

Correct Process Failure

When the investigation or charging process shows signs of misconduct, the court has a duty to confront it rather than normalize it. The court recognized Bartholomew had "overstepped her bounds" — then did nothing.

The "Level Playing Field" Illusion

Judges are supposed to be neutral referees. The rules of evidence are supposed to apply equally to both sides. But in State v. Allen, the trial transcript reveals a consistent and disturbing asymmetry: when the prosecution stumbled, the judge bent over backward to help; when the defense sought to explore context, the judge nailed the law squarely and shut the door.

When the State's evidence is threatened by a technicality, the judge feels justified in relaxing the rules to "get to the truth." But when the Defense tries to explore an alternative truth, the judge suddenly becomes a strict constructionist, using the rules of evidence as a shield to protect the State's narrative from being challenged.
— The Level Playing Field Illusion

When the Prosecution Stumbles

  • The judge invents a non-hearsay exception to admit blatant hearsay without the prosecutor stating a purpose.
  • The judge uses an "illustrative purposes" workaround to admit photos lacking foundation.
  • The judge supplies the missing theory of authentication for Exhibit 4 when the State cannot establish authorship.
  • The standard is loose, accommodating, and focused on letting the emotional narrative proceed.
versus

When the Defense Seeks Context

  • The judge strictly enforces rules of form, sustaining objections to "broad" defense questions.
  • The defense is forced to be hyper-specific, technically perfect, and confined to a microscopic scope.
  • The standard is rigid, unforgiving, and focused on shutting down narrative exploration.
The prosecution gets to paint with a broad brush. The defense is forced to paint with a tiny one. This asymmetry creates a profound imbalance in how the story of the trial is told — and the jury sees it.
— The Structural Imbalance

The Practical Consequences

1

Prosecution Broad Brush

Emotional, third-party perceptions and loosely authenticated evidence build a cohesive, compelling narrative for the jury.

2

Defense Tiny Brush

Held to the absolute strictest letter of the law. Prevented from exploring context or alternative explanations unless perfectly framed.

3

The defense looks evasive, nitpicky, or unable to offer a coherent alternative story — while the State's narrative flows unimpeded.

This is not merely a complaint about losing evidentiary rulings. It is a structural observation about how judicial bias operates in practice. The trial court consistently relaxed the rules of evidence to permit the State to introduce prejudicial, uncross-examined, and emotionally charged testimony, while simultaneously applying a hyper-strict, technically unforgiving standard to the Defense's attempts to elicit relevant contextual information. This asymmetrical application of the rules deprived the Defendant of a fair trial before a neutral arbiter and prevented the jury from hearing a complete and balanced presentation of the facts.

The Happel Colloquy: The Factual Record

Steven Happel was a State's witness — called by the prosecution, not the defense. Before trial, the prosecution attempted to pressure him not to appear. He ignored those threats and came anyway. On the morning of trial, before Happel was called to testify, defense counsel brought to the court's attention that Happel had reported an improper contact with the prosecution team. The court conducted an in-chambers colloquy, placed Happel under oath, and took the following sworn account.

In-Chambers Colloquy — Sworn Testimony of Steven Happel

State v. Benjamin Allen • Trial Morning • Under Oath
1 THE COURT Mr. Happel, you've been placed under oath. Please describe what you observed yesterday in the courthouse lobby.
2 HAPPEL I was told to present myself at 2 p.m. yesterday in the old courthouse, and I waited in the lobby. I did go out to my car to kind of relax. I came back in. I talked to the investigator Scott. I don't know his last name. I forgot. That's the only person I talked to about the case, answering questions.
3 HAPPEL And as I was sitting in the main lobby around 2 to 3 p.m. yesterday, Ms. Bartholomew and the victim and other witnesses, maybe, or her family walked in through the front door and were talking about the trial and how the defendant pleaded or was going to plead, something like that.
4 HAPPEL And then I heard Ms. Bartholomew tell the victim that she should have said that the victim was afraid of the witnesses, which is me and Joseph DeMarco.
5 HAPPEL And I felt intimidated, maybe like my feelings or thought process was trying to be played with, and felt very uncomfortable.
6 THE COURT Did you confront Ms. Bartholomew about what you heard?
7 HAPPEL No, sir. I did not.
8 THE COURT Do you believe this incident will affect your testimony here today?
9 HAPPEL The only answers I'll give are truthful answers that I can recall.
10 THE COURT I'm satisfied this witness is prepared to tell the truth, the whole truth and nothing but the truth, as he indicated under his oath. So we'll go ahead, then, bring in the jury at this time.
The court heard a sworn account of prosecutorial witness-coaching in the courthouse lobby — and its sole response was to ask the witness if he'd still tell the truth.
— The Framing Mistake

Happel further testified that he did not confront Bartholomew at the time, that he believed the incident would affect the trial, and that the ethical question "kind of disturbed" him. The court asked whether he believed the contact would affect his testimony. Happel replied: "The only answers I'll give are truthful answers that I can recall."

The jury was brought in. Happel testified for the State — without curative instruction, without investigation of the coaching allegation, and without sanction or admonishment of ADA Bartholomew.

The Hearsay Double Standard: When the Judge Became the Prosecutor's Advocate

The asymmetry in evidentiary rulings was not subtle. It was captured with precision in the trial transcript. One exchange in particular crystallizes the problem — the judge's handling of a hearsay objection that reveals exactly how the "level playing field" collapsed.

The Hearsay That Wasn't — Trial Testimony of Lauren Deal

Direct Examination • State v. Allen
1 LAUREN DEAL I feel like he was just as scared as I was. I mean, you know, in the car ride to the hospital, you know, he had said that he would not let anything else happen to me. He would not take me back over there. His girlfriend had been through something similar, and his exact words were, my girlfriend would kill me if I didn't help you.
2 MR. WUNSCH Objection, your honor.
3 THE COURT Well, clearly not being offered for the truth of the matter asserted, so I'll overrule the objection.
4 MS. BARTHOLOMEW [No argument offered. No purpose stated.]
The judge essentially acted as an advocate for the prosecution by supplying the legal justification out of thin air. The prosecutor did not make any argument or state a purpose for the evidence.
— Procedural Error Analysis

Why the Objection Was Valid — And Why the Ruling Failed

Textbook Hearsay

The witness testified about out-of-court statements made by a third party (the driver). Under N.C. R. Evid. 801, this is the textbook definition of hearsay. The judge overruled by claiming the statements were "clearly not being offered for the truth" — but the prosecutor never articulated any non-hearsay purpose.

"Effect on the Listener" Fails Here

A victim's subjective feeling of safety during a car ride to the hospital is generally irrelevant to whether the defendant committed the alleged assault. The statement "my girlfriend would kill me if I didn't help you" was offered to explain why the driver helped — but a bystander's motive for helping is entirely irrelevant to the defendant's guilt.

The "Trojan Horse" of Implicit Validation

By allowing the statement, the prosecution subtly used the driver as an unsworn character witness to validate the victim's story. The implicit message: "Even this bystander recognized how serious this was." This is classic bolstering — using a third party's untested perception to reinforce the narrative without calling that third party to testify.

Deprivation of Fair Opportunity to Respond

When a judge supplies the rationale for the prosecutor, it deprives the defense of a fair opportunity to object to that specific rationale. If the prosecutor had actually argued "effect on the listener," defense counsel could have immediately countered that the listener's state of mind was not a material issue. By jumping in, the judge short-circuited the adversarial process.

What Should Have Happened

The Procedurally Correct Sequence

How a Neutral Judge Would Have Handled the Objection
1 MR. WUNSCH Objection, your honor. Hearsay.
2 THE COURT Ms. Bartholomew, what is the non-hearsay purpose for which this statement is being offered? [The judge puts the burden back on the prosecutor.]
3 MS. BARTHOLOMEW [Either states a valid, relevant purpose, or remains silent.]
4 THE COURT Objection sustained. The jury will disregard the witness's last statement. [If no valid purpose is stated.]

By skipping the critical step — requiring the prosecutor to articulate a valid purpose — and inventing the rationale herself, the judge committed a clear error in evidentiary procedure. Worse, the judge should have struck the testimony and given a curative instruction, because the prejudicial statements were already in the record. No such instruction was ever given. The jury heard the driver's emotional validation and was left to weigh it as though it were properly admitted evidence.

The Court's Failure — And What Should Have Happened

The trial court's response can be summarized in three sentences: it held a colloquy, found Happel credible enough to testify, and brought the jury in. That is all. No investigation. No mistrial. No disqualification. No curative instruction. No referral to the State Bar.

The Framing Mistake

The court treated the incident as a credibility question — "Are you prepared to tell the truth?" — rather than an ethical crisis requiring structural intervention. This converted a prosecutorial misconduct problem into a witness reliability problem. The question was not whether Happel would tell the truth; it was whether the State's case had been manufactured before the jury ever sat down. The court's own language recognized that Bartholomew had "overstepped her bounds" — yet the remedy was nothing.

The alleged conduct — a prosecutor coaching the complaining witness to fabricate fear of the State's own witnesses — is among the most serious forms of prosecutorial misconduct. Four remedies were available and required:

A. Mistrial or Continuance

Where a prosecutor is alleged to have coached a witness, the contamination is not curable by a simple instruction — the testimony is tainted at its source. See N.C. Gen. Stat. § 15A-1061. A mistrial, or at minimum a continuance to permit investigation and potential substitution of counsel, was the constitutionally appropriate response.

B. Prosecutor Disqualification

North Carolina courts have authority to disqualify a prosecutor whose conduct violates the rules of professional conduct or deprives the defendant of a fair trial. See N.C. RPC 3.8, N.C. RPC 8.4. Directing a witness to fabricate an emotional response is precisely the conduct requiring disqualification. No step was taken.

C. Curative Instruction

Even without a mistrial or disqualification, the court was obligated to instruct the jury that an attorney's attempt to influence witness testimony is improper, and that testimony aligning with litigation strategy rather than independent recollection should be scrutinized. No such instruction was given.

D. Referral to Oversight Authorities

The court was obligated to report the misconduct to the North Carolina State Bar. See N.C. RPC 3.3, N.C. RPC 8.3. The State Bar had already received complaints about Bartholomew and taken no action. The court's failure to report ensured the Bar's file remained incomplete — and its inaction insulated.

The Pattern Beyond Happel

The coaching incident was not isolated. It was one thread in a fabric of evidentiary manipulation spanning the entire prosecution.

State's Exhibit 4 — The Authentication Failure

December 3, 2016 Facebook Messages • Conditional Admission • Rule 104(b)
1 MS. BARTHOLOMEW Your Honor, at this time, the State would introduce State's Exhibit No. 4.
2 MR. WUNSCH Objection. In this message, it clearly states — this is not Ben. It's Matt. I'm using his phone. And the last message states later that Ben says, I wasn't even — I didn't even have possession of my phone. Matt had my phone. So it's not Ben. It's clearly in the text; it's not Ben. We're picking out one page.
3 THE COURT [After off-record bench discussion and jury removal] If she testifies that they had a discussion prior to December 3, 2016, at 1:49, then that would give this particular page context. They will need to link this up with a conversation prior to December 3, 2016.
4 THE COURT [After jury returned] Ladies and gentlemen, in my discretion, I have overruled the objection, found the proposed exhibit number four to be admissible and sufficiently reliable to be admitted.
The judge supplied the missing theory of authentication for the prosecution. The condition was never satisfied — but the evidence remained before the jury.
— Exhibit 4 Analysis

The Authentication Problem

The exhibit's own content identified the sender as "Matt" using the defendant's phone. The defendant himself stated he did not have possession of his phone. Under N.C. R. Evid. 901, authentication is a condition precedent to admissibility. The displayed account name does not automatically identify the person operating the device — especially when the State's own exhibit shows that control of the account was disputed.

The Judge Supplied the Missing Theory

Instead of requiring the prosecution to resolve the defect through competent evidence, the judge developed a conditional theory: if the witness testified to a prior bonfire conversation before 1:49 p.m., the message could be connected to Allen. The judge did not merely evaluate evidence — the judge identified the exact additional fact that could salvage the State's deficient foundation.

The Condition Was Never Satisfied

The ADA asked about bonfire history ("maybe five") but never asked whether the witness and Allen had discussed that particular bonfire before 1:49 p.m. on December 3, 2016. A history of attending bonfires does not establish the specific prior conversation on which the court based its authentication ruling. The condition remained unsatisfied — yet the exhibit stayed in evidence.

The ADA Exceeded the Court's Limitation

The judge stated the State was "simply trying to introduce the very first one." But the ADA elicited the witness's response, its timestamp, a further statement attributed to Allen, a photograph of a bonfire, and another response — expanding the evidence beyond the single message used to justify the ruling. The court allowed it without correction.

The judge told the jury the exhibit was "sufficiently reliable." That was not a neutral evidentiary ruling. It was an endorsement of the State's disputed evidence — addressing the very factual issue the defense disputed.
— Judicial Endorsement of Disputed Evidence

The Rule of Completeness Violation

The State offered only the first page of the Facebook exchange. Later pages identified another user of the phone and undermined the attribution the State was attempting to prove. Under N.C. R. Evid. 106, the defense was entitled to have the complete context admitted contemporaneously — not as a separate defense exhibit offered later. The judge recognized this problem yet placed the burden of completeness on the defense, allowing the State to create the initial narrative and requiring the defense to undo it after the jury had already heard the prosecution's version.

Why This Matters: The Cumulative Harm

The prejudice from Exhibit 4 was not confined to the words in the messages. The ruling affected the jury's perception of the entire trial. The jurors saw the defense raise a specific, fact-based objection; the judge reject the defense's position; the judge describe the State's evidence as reliable; the prosecutor proceed beyond the stated limitation; and no judicial correction when the required foundation failed to materialize. The implicit message was powerful: the State's evidence was trustworthy, the defense's objections lacked merit, and the prosecutor had the court's approval.

DeMarco's Distorted Statement

Freeman's affidavit claimed DeMarco "made the decision to get his vehicle and ride in an attempt to locate her." DeMarco's recorded statement says he got afraid and left — he incidentally saw Deal while driving. Freeman upgraded a chance encounter into a deliberate search. Deal said she hid behind a tree; DeMarco said she ran through a field. Both cannot be true. The affidavit was used for probable cause and never corrected.

Illustrative Evidence Without Foundation

The State offered photos of the woods and residence during Deal's direct examination. The court overruled foundation objections, admitted them "for illustrative purposes," and permitted Deal to step down from the stand to point at locations — a performative walkthrough under conditions she claimed were complete darkness and unfamiliar terrain. The bar video, which would have provided objective documentation, went unused.

The Cumulative Effect

Each ruling was framed in terms of discretion — "illustrative purposes," "sufficiently reliable" — but the cumulative effect was to lower the State's evidentiary burden at precisely the points where the record was weakest. The court did not merely err in individual rulings; it created a procedural environment in which the State could present a manufactured case with minimal judicial scrutiny.

The "Misunderstanding": Plea Negotiation Misrepresentation

During plea discussions, ADA Bartholomew represented to the defendant: "We found your sperm and DNA on her." This representation was false. The SBI report, dated May 11, 2020, stated: "No spermatozoa present." The Y-STR analysis found a non-individualizing profile matching 1 in 518 Caucasian males — including all paternal relatives — a result that could not be attributed to the defendant with any specificity.

A prosecutor with ten years of sexual assault experience, a district court judgeship, Secret Service training, and UNC certification in evidence interpretation does not "misunderstand" whether sperm evidence exists.

— Analysis of the "Misunderstanding" Claim

The Connective Tissue

The lie during plea negotiations is directly tied to the Happel coaching incident and the Exhibit 4 authentication failure. All three events reflect the same prosecutorial disposition: to use the tools of the courtroom — witness testimony, electronic evidence, forensic claims — in ways that the record does not support, and to rely on procedural leniency when those tools are challenged. The "misunderstanding" explanation is the connective tissue: it reveals a prosecutor who expects to be forgiven for overreach because the system is designed to protect her from consequences.

The Consequences: What the Coaching Produced

The Linguistic Shift

Forensic linguistic analysis comparing Deal's 2019 statements to her 2023 trial testimony reveals cosine similarity collapsing from 1.0 to 0.28. The shift is directional:

  • She began claiming she "froze" — a term absent from her 2019 interviews.
  • She began invoking "fear of witnesses" — precisely what Bartholomew was heard coaching.
  • Her vocabulary shifted from concrete ("truck," "bathroom," "keys") to legalistic ("force," "fear," "trust," "freezing," "triggers").

This is not natural memory evolution. It is the signature of coached testimony: the witness moves from "what happened" to "what the jury needs to hear."

The Jury's Answer

The jury acquitted Benjamin Allen of first-degree forcible rape 12-0 — a unanimous rejection of the charge most dependent on the coached narrative. The hung jury on the lesser charge does not validate the prosecution; it reflects the impossibility of achieving unanimity when the evidence is compromised and the witness has been coached. A prosecutor who manufactures testimony creates a fragile foundation that cannot sustain a unanimous conviction on the primary charge.

Bench Book Reference: What the Judge Should Have Consulted

A trial judge confronted with alleged prosecutorial witness-coaching does not need to invent the response. The authorities below define the minimum standard. None were followed.

North Carolina Judicial Bench Book — Applicable Authorities

N.C. Gen. Stat. § 15A-1061

Mistrial authority. Where prosecutor is alleged to have coached a witness, contamination is not curable by simple instruction — testimony is tainted at its source.

N.C. RPC 3.8

Special responsibilities of a prosecutor. The prosecutor is a minister of justice whose duty is to seek justice, not merely to obtain or preserve a conviction.

N.C. RPC 8.4

Professional misconduct. Directing a witness to fabricate an emotional response to defense witnesses is precisely the type of conduct requiring disqualification and discipline.

N.C. RPC 3.3 & 8.3

Candor toward tribunal and reporting professional misconduct. The court was obligated to report the misconduct to the North Carolina State Bar and judicial oversight authorities.

N.C. R. Evid. 901

Authentication requirement. The proponent must produce evidence sufficient to support a finding that the item is what the proponent claims it is. A displayed account name does not automatically identify the person operating the device.

N.C. R. Evid. 104(b)

Conditional relevance. Evidence admitted subject to later introduction of connecting fact — but the required connecting evidence must actually be introduced. The rule does not authorize retention of the benefit after failing to establish the condition.

N.C. R. Evid. 106

Rule of Completeness. When a writing or recorded statement is introduced, the adverse party may require introduction of any other part that in fairness ought to be considered at the same time.

N.C. R. Evid. 403

Exclusion of relevant evidence on grounds of prejudice, confusion, or waste of time. Even marginally relevant evidence must be excluded if probative value is substantially outweighed by danger of unfair prejudice.

The court did not hold the State to the authentication condition it imposed. It did not hold the ADA to the scope it announced. It did not ensure that the account-sharing context accompanied the State's selected evidence. It did not refrain from endorsing the reliability of disputed evidence before the jury. At each decision point, the benefit went to the prosecution.
— Summary of Judicial Failures

What Relief Is Required

1. State Bar Referral

Formal investigation of ADA Bartholomew under N.C. RPC 3.3, 3.4, 3.8, and 8.4 for the Happel coaching, Exhibit 4 authentication failure, DeMarco statement distortion, and plea negotiation misrepresentation.

2. Judicial Standards Commission

Referral of the trial judge for failure to report known prosecutorial misconduct, overruling authentication and hearsay objections without adequate foundation, and failure to take remedial action when misconduct was brought to the court's attention under oath.

3. Case Review

Review of all cases prosecuted by Bartholomew, with attention to witness preparation, lobby conversations with witnesses, late-stage narrative shifts, conditional admission of electronic evidence, and plea negotiations involving forensic misrepresentations.

4. Record Preservation & Transparency

Preserve the Happel colloquy transcript, linguistic analysis, Exhibit 4 foundation hearings, and DeMarco recordings as part of the official record. The Lee County Clerk and AOC should publish a complete index of all complaints, investigations, and disciplinary actions related to this case.

Epilogue

The Happel incident is not a story about what happened during a trial. It is a story about what the system permitted to happen and then chose to ignore. A witness came forward under oath. He reported that the prosecutor had tried to shape his testimony and the complainant's testimony. The court heard him, thanked him, and sent him to the stand. The State Bar was introduced to the prosecutor's file and did nothing. The system worked exactly as designed: it protected the process from disruption, and it protected the prosecutor from accountability.

The defendant was acquitted of the primary charge — a testament to the jury's ability to see through manufactured testimony. But acquittal is not justice. Justice requires a clean process, uncoached witnesses, properly authenticated evidence, honest prosecutors, and officers of the court held to the standards they impose on everyone else. None of those things happened here.

The Happel incident remains in the record as a documented coaching attempt with no consequence. Exhibit 4 was admitted without the State satisfying the court's own condition. DeMarco's statement was distorted. The ADA lied about sperm evidence and called it a misunderstanding. The system protected the process from disruption. It protected the prosecutor from accountability.

It left the defendant to wonder whether justice is anything more than a word we use to describe the outcome when the machinery of the state decides to stop grinding.
