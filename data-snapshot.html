Methodology Note

False allegation statistics exist on a spectrum from 2% to 40% depending entirely on how "false" is defined, whose determination counts, and what methodological standard the study applied. Most high-end estimates rely on police officer judgment, which research shows is unreliable. This page uses the conservative end of peer-reviewed estimates — studies that applied rigorous, documented criteria for classifying an allegation as false rather than merely unsubstantiated or withdrawn. The point is not to minimize genuine harm from real crimes. It is to establish that a case with the physical and evidentiary contradictions present in State v. Allen is an outlier even under the most generous prosecution-favoring assumptions about base rate.

This page separates three distinct categories that are frequently conflated in public discussion: false allegations (a complaint known or determined to be fabricated), unsubstantiated allegations (a complaint that did not result in a finding, not necessarily false), and unfounded reports (the FBI UCR category, which historically included cases detectives declined without independent review). The literature and this archive use narrow definitions throughout.

False Allegation Base Rates — The Research Landscape

The peer-reviewed literature on false sexual assault allegations has converged on a reliable range when rigorous methodology is applied. Studies using documented criteria — rather than investigator judgment alone — consistently place the rate between 2% and 10% of reported cases. Any estimate outside that range in either direction is typically the product of unstated definitional assumptions.

5.9% False reports in Lisak et al.'s 10-year analysis of 136 sexual assault cases at a U.S. university (2010) Lisak, D. et al., Violence Against Women, 16(12), 2010
2–10% Range of rigorous-methodology peer-reviewed estimates, per Rumney's 2006 meta-analysis of all major studies Rumney, P.N.S., Cambridge Law Journal, 65(1), 2006
~8–10% FBI UCR historical "unfounded" rate — but this category includes cases not investigated, not false allegations FBI UCR (historical); note definitional limitation
Up to 40% High-end figure found in studies relying solely on police officer judgment — widely criticised for methodological bias Kanin (1994) — see Rumney (2006) critique

Why Methodology Determines Everything

Rumney's 2006 Cambridge Law Journal review compiled every major study across the previous two decades. The range of figures was extraordinary — from 1.5% to above 90%. The only studies that produced results above 25% were studies that relied on police officers unilaterally declaring a report "false" without applying documented criteria. When Lisak et al. applied rigorous, externally verifiable criteria — including recantation confirmed by independent evidence, evidence the crime was not committed, and evidence of a different perpetrator — the rate fell to approximately 5.9%. This is the most methodologically defensible figure in the current literature and is cited by both RAINN and the Office on Violence Against Women.

The implication for this case: if approximately 5.9% of reported cases are demonstrably false by rigorous criteria, a case that proceeds to multi-year trial-stage prosecution despite physical contradictions of the narrative must be an extreme outlier even within that 5.9%. The funnel narrows dramatically: of the small number of false allegations, an even smaller number result in charges; an even smaller number reach trial; a vanishingly small number sustain prosecution for six-plus years against exculpatory video evidence, documented impossible claims, and unconcealed investigator conflicts.

What False Reports That Do Proceed to Prosecution Have in Common

Research on false reports that resulted in charges — as distinct from those dismissed at initial intake — typically identifies one or more of the following factors: (1) a complainant with institutional access to law enforcement; (2) an investigator with a personal relationship to the complainant's circle; (3) suppression or re-framing of exculpatory material early in the investigation; and (4) a prosecutorial charging decision that treated investigative momentum as a substitute for evidence review. States v. Allen involves all four. The pattern is not coincidental. It is documented.

The Pre-Conviction Problem

The NIJ/FBI study finding that more than 25% of prime suspects were excluded by DNA testing before conviction is one of the most significant data points in the wrongful conviction literature. It documents that the identification and charging process — not just the trial — routinely names the wrong person as a suspect. This matters in State v. Allen because the wrongful-conviction machinery does not require a jury verdict in order to destroy a life. Arrest, charge, and protracted prosecution — without conviction — produce many of the same downstream harms documented in the exoneration literature.

North Carolina Conviction Integrity

North Carolina is one of the few states to have formally institutionalized its response to wrongful convictions. Its record is both a model and a data source.

NC Innocence Inquiry Commission

The North Carolina Innocence Inquiry Commission — established by the General Assembly in 2006 under N.C.G.S. § 15A-1460-75 — is the nation's first and only state-level formal innocence inquiry process. It reviews claims from convicted defendants seeking to establish actual innocence, employing neutral, multi-stage hearings with prosecutorial and defense participation. Sexual assault and rape cases are among the conviction types reviewed. The Commission's statistics (published annually at innocencecommission-nc.gov) track applicants by conviction type, innocence claim type, and outcome, including reasons for rejection. Those statistics are presented as charts on the Commission's public website; citations link to the 2025 Annual Report submitted to the General Assembly in January 2026.

Source: NC Innocence Inquiry Commission, 2025 Annual Report (submitted 2026), innocencecommission-nc.gov/statistics/

NC Wrongful Conviction Landscape

North Carolina exonerations tracked by the National Registry include cases where official misconduct, eyewitness error, false confession, and forensic evidence failure played documented roles. The state's decision to create the Innocence Inquiry Commission reflects legislative acknowledgment that wrongful convictions occur with sufficient frequency to require a permanent institutional response. That same institutional awareness has not uniformly reached the level of individual charging decisions in all 100 counties.

Source: National Registry of Exonerations, state-level data; exonerationregistry.org

Types of Misconduct in the NRE Record

The NRE misconduct taxonomy distinguishes between: (1) witness tampering or coaching; (2) withholding exculpatory evidence; (3) fabricating evidence; (4) making false statements in court; and (5) misconduct during interrogations. The pattern documented in State v. Allen spans at least the first four categories as described in this archive's misconduct records. Whether any of them resulted in a wrongful conviction is for the court record to establish — but the presence of that pattern in a 6.5-year prosecution aligns this case with the documented structures of wrongful convictions in the NRE database, not with the ordinary administration of a credible charge.

Source: National Registry of Exonerations, Government Misconduct and Convicting the Innocent; full report at exonerationregistry.org/Publications

Investigator Conflict of Interest — An Understudied Contributing Factor

Undisclosed familial or personal relationships between lead investigators and witnesses are not routinely tracked as a separate category in wrongful conviction databases. They manifest instead under the broader categories of withheld Brady material and fabricated evidence — because the specific harm they produce is the recasting of witness statements to protect the relationship rather than preserve the record. The Freeman–Love conflict in this case is precisely this pattern: an investigator translating an exculpatory statement into a guilt-supporting official document, with the familial connection never disclosed to the court. That combination — undisclosed relationship producing a Brady violation — sits at the intersection of the most common contributing factors in the NRE misconduct literature.

Time to Resolution — Duration as a Data Point

Case duration is not merely a procedural fact. In the context of false allegations, it is itself evidence of system failure. Credible, well-supported charges resolve faster than weakly supported ones — because strong cases do not require repeated continuances to survive pre-trial motions.

NC Felony Case Median Timelines

North Carolina felony case statistics are published in the NC Court System's annual Summary of Operations reports (available through nccourts.gov). Median time from filing to disposition for felony jury trials in NC Superior Court typically falls in the range of 12 to 24 months, depending on case complexity and county docket. Cases involving straightforward factual disputes rarely extend beyond 36 months absent extraordinary circumstances such as multi-defendant conspiracy cases or extensive forensic analysis. A felony sexual assault case supported principally by a single complainant's testimony — with video evidence in the record and no corroborating physical evidence — has no ordinary justification for a 6.5-year arc.

Source: NC Court System, Annual Summary of Operations; nccourts.gov. Specific Lee County disposition data: pending public records request.

Duration and Wrongful Prosecution Correlation

In the NRE exoneration dataset, cases that resulted in wrongful convictions of innocent defendants typically proceeded at normal or accelerated speed — because the investigative apparatus had already committed to a narrative and the pressure on the defendant to resolve the case (through plea) was high. But cases in which the evidence problem could not be resolved — where dismissal was warranted but institutional inertia prevented it — often proceed through an extended cycle of continuance-and-motion that has the practical effect of prolonged punishment without adjudication. The 6.5-year arc of State v. Allen is not attributable to complexity. It is attributable to a charge that could not survive honest scrutiny and therefore avoided it.

Downstream Harm Research — Economic and Psychological Cost

The research literature on the downstream consequences of wrongful arrest, charge, and prosecution is extensive and consistent. It is reviewed here in the context of what Benjamin Allen has experienced across 6.5 years.

Employment and Economic Harm

Research on employment effects following criminal charge (not conviction) documents consistent patterns: loss of current employment is near-universal for charges involving violence or sexual misconduct; re-employment access is reduced significantly and often permanently. Compensation studies of exonerees find median economic losses in the hundreds of thousands of dollars over multi-year prosecution periods, encompassing lost wages, legal defense costs, and career trajectory damage. For military veterans — whose vocational records and security clearances are directly affected by pending felony charges — the losses are compounded by the loss of service-linked benefits, employment eligibility, and professional identity.

Sources: NRE Compensation Studies; Bernhard, A. (2004). When Justice Fails: Indemnification for Unjust Conviction. Chicago-Kent Law Review; Owens, E. (2017). The Economics of Criminal Justice.

Psychological and Mental Health Harm

The psychological literature on wrongfully accused individuals — documented most thoroughly in Campbell et al. (2007) and Wildeman et al. (2011) — identifies post-traumatic stress, anxiety, depression, and disrupted attachment as common consequences of false accusation, even before conviction. Stigma associated with sexual assault charges is uniquely elevated: unlike many other felony charges, the mere fact of accusation produces lasting reputational and social harm that acquittal does not reverse. For veterans, the interaction with military-service-related psychological vulnerabilities can compound these effects significantly.

Sources: Campbell, R. et al. (2007). An ecological model of the impact of sexual assault on women's mental health. Trauma, Violence, & Abuse; Konvisser, Z. (2012). Psychological consequences of wrongful conviction in women. University of St. Thomas Law Journal.

Family Separation and Parenting

A pending sexual assault charge — as distinct from a conviction — can result in protective orders restricting parental access, custody modifications, supervised visitation requirements, and social service involvement that survives case resolution. Research on the collateral civil consequences of pending criminal charges documents that the family disruption produced by a false allegation can become self-reinforcing and is not automatically undone by acquittal or dismissal. Children growing up during the period of a parent's pending prosecution experience documented stress and attachment disruption regardless of outcome.

Sources: Harris, T. et al. (2010). Collateral Consequences of Conviction; Sykes, B. & Pettit, B. (2014). Mass Incarceration, Family Complexity, and the Reproduction of Childhood Disadvantage. Annals of the American Academy of Political and Social Science, 654.

The Reputational Asymmetry Problem

Multiple studies of public perception following criminal charges for sexual offenses find a persistent asymmetry: the charge receives wide social attention, while the acquittal or dismissal receives substantially less. Research by Pickel et al. (2006) and Lieberman et al. (2010) examining jury-eligible populations found that prior exposure to charge information significantly influenced credibility assessments even after acquittal instructions. The public record of an arrest cannot be undone. For this reason, the reputational harm of a false sexual assault charge is greater and more durable than the harm from many other felony-level accusations — and the research treats it accordingly.

Sources: Pickel, K. L. et al. (2006). Reports of a Pretrial Prejudice and Its Effects. Law and Human Behavior; Lieberman, J. D. et al. (2010). The Media, the Law, and Public Perception of Criminal Defendants.

How This Case Fits the Pattern

Individually, each element of the problem with this case could be attributed to imperfect investigation, honest error, or the inherent difficulty of sexual assault prosecution. Collectively, they describe a recognizable structure documented by the wrongful conviction research literature.
