Traditionally, Lady Justice carries scales to signify deliberation, proportionality, and the weighing of evidence, and a sword to signify the authority to enforce judgment once that weighing is complete. The sequence matters. Reason precedes force. Judgment is earned before it is imposed unless you live in Lee County, North Carolina.

The Sheriff's Office breaks that sequence entirely. In Lee County, the representation of Lady Justice is very different. This image represents how law enforcement operates and what is accepted by the legal community.

What follows is not allegory but accusation, currently lived by a former Lee County citizen.

Judgment without balance.
The absence of the scales removes the symbol of evidentiary restraint. There is no weighing, no calibration, no pause. Two swords signify decisions rendered without proportional assessment—outcomes driven by certainty, assertion, or institutional confidence rather than proof. Justice no longer measures. It advances.

Force becomes self-reinforcing.
One sword represents authority constrained by law. Two swords represent authority reinforcing itself. Enforcement stacked upon enforcement. Power justified by its own repetition. This is not law backed by legitimacy; it is coercion backed by momentum.

Neutrality abandoned.
The scales signify impartiality. Their absence, paired with dual weapons, transforms justice from an arbiter into a combatant. The posture is no longer neutral; it is adversarial. There are no competing claims to be weighed—only outcomes to be imposed. Justice is no longer an umpire. It has chosen a side – especially when the family of law enforcement is trying to make some extra money.

Institutional self-authorship.
The book beneath her is no longer revered; it is altered. We the People is crossed out. In its place stands an institutional name, sketched rather than engraved, implying authorship without consent. The foundational text is not destroyed—it is overwritten. Authority no longer flows from the governed upward; it circulates internally, citing itself as its own warrant.

Restraint is not lost—it is rejected.
The blindfold remains, but its meaning has changed. It no longer symbolizes impartiality; it symbolizes indifference. The system does not fail to see. It chooses not to look. The torn flag behind her confirms the cost: legitimacy frayed, ideals exhausted, symbols retained only as backdrop.

Scales ask.
Swords enforce.

This image does not depict justice in crisis.
It depicts a justice system that believes it no longer needs permission.
A STATEMENT OF INDICTMENT

The New Face
of Injustice

Meet Assistant District Attorney Tiffany Bartholomew and Detective Steve Freeman

If Mike Nifong became the symbol of a prosecutor who betrayed justice to preserve a false narrative, then Tiffany Bartholomew must become the symbol of something far more insidious: a prosecutor who did not merely protect a lie — she helped build it, shape it, coach it, polish it, and arm it for battle in open court.

When the facts failed, the questions changed.
When the truth became inconvenient, emotion was substituted.
When the evidence refused to carry the State where it wanted to go, pressure, coaching, and outright manipulation filled the void.

That is not prosecution.
That is narrative engineering.

That is not witness preparation.
That is witness construction.

Bartholomew did not merely ready the accuser for the stand — she rewrote the accuser's entire vocabulary, transforming raw accusation into courtroom-ready testimony. She crossed every line: switching from fact-based inquiry to emotional theater the moment manufactured evidence and coached lies could no longer bridge the gap. She intimidated witnesses in the courthouse and on joint calls with her detective. There was no boundary she would not breach.

This is not preparation. This is fabrication with the full authority of the State behind it.
And Detective Steve Freeman? He is the perfect partner in this enterprise — the investigator who treats facts as optional, procedure as optional, and accountability as a punchline reserved for defendants but never for the State.
  • • No urgency to secure the scene.
  • • No urgency to preserve evidence.
  • • No urgency to test the story against physical reality.
  • • No concern for contradictions. No concern for conflicts of interest.

He attended a family wedding instead of investigating. Crime-scene diagrams? Beneath him. Weapons checks? Safety assessments? Immediate collection of physical evidence? All dismissed. Statements taken from witnesses were altered in his official reports — even though the original recorded versions were handed straight to the defense. On the stand he lied about the arrest process, claiming a warrant existed when the magistrate's order — sitting in plain view — proved he had none.

He did it because he knew, with absolute certainty,
that nothing would happen.

Nothing ever does.

Bartholomew was there to cover for him. She crafts the falsehoods; he delivers them. Subornation of perjury is not a scandal to her — it is a tool. The only problem she would have is if her witnesses lacked the stomach to perjure themselves for a conviction.

This is what makes Bartholomew and Freeman more dangerous than Nifong ever was.
Nifong became a scandal. His name became a stain. The system at least pretended to be shocked.

But these two — and the institution that shields them — have learned the real lesson from Durham: next time, be smarter about hiding it. Coach more carefully. Shade the facts more confidently. Close ranks faster.

They acted with the calm assurance that the lines no longer matter — because the system has already decided to protect its own once it commits to a false story. The defendant complained to counsel. Letters went to the Bar. Contradictions were laid bare in the record. The machinery never slowed.

That is the public scandal here: not merely that falsehood was advanced, but that the entire apparatus of justice appeared willing to absorb it, defend it, and carry it forward as though truth were irrelevant.

Nifong's disgrace at least became visible. Bartholomew and Freeman represent the next, more corrosive stage — when betrayal of justice is no longer reckless but disciplined; no longer shocking but routine; no longer hidden because it cannot be seen, but because the institutions that see it refuse to act.

This is what makes Bartholomew and Freeman more dangerous than Nifong ever was.
Nifong became a scandal. His name became a stain. The system at least pretended to be shocked.

Mike Nifong should have been a warning that misconduct would not be tolerated. Instead, the 12th Prosecutorial District used his story as a training manual.

This is not a procedural footnote.
This is not an academic debate for law reviews.

Because when prosecutors become storytellers and investigators become stagehands — when the machinery of justice exists only to control the narrative rather than discover the truth — then every citizen becomes a potential target of extortion by those who wear badges or carry the State's seal.

The system's legitimacy does not rest on the mythical perfection of its actors.
It rests on their demonstrated willingness to police their own.
When that willingness dies, the foundation does not merely crack.
It crumbles.

We are watching it crumble in real time.

Truth still matters.
Process still protects.
Power must still answer to principle.

Or we no longer have a justice system —
we have a story factory with uniforms and gavels.

And in Lee County,
the machines are running at full speed.
