Cognitive Rationalizations in Misconduct

Law enforcement officers and prosecutors often rely on cognitive rationalizations that sanitize misconduct and recast deviant conduct as necessary, harmless, or justified. Those rationalizations can operate before misconduct, by preempting guilt, and after misconduct, by neutralizing regret. Over time, they reshape the moral boundaries of an institution.



Denial of Victim: The target is framed as deserving mistreatment. In a criminal case, that often appears as the view that a defendant is unworthy of fair dealing, full disclosure, or procedural protection.



Denial of Responsibility: Misconduct is recast as unavoidable. Officers attribute it to pressure, culture, or necessity; prosecutors attribute it to caseload, politics, or the perceived need to “make the case stick.”



Denial of Injury: The actor insists no real harm occurred. That mindset often appears when suppressed evidence is dismissed as immaterial or when misleading testimony is treated as inconsequential.



Social Weighting: Misconduct is minimized by comparison to worse scandals elsewhere. That rhetorical move makes serious violations appear ordinary or trivial.



Moral Justification: Rule-breaking is framed as necessary to accomplish a higher good. This is the most dangerous rationalization because it does not merely excuse misconduct; it portrays it as virtuous.



Institutional Significance

When these rationalizations become normalized, misconduct stops appearing exceptional and begins to appear righteous. In that environment, unlawful conduct is not merely tolerated. It is rewarded, repeated, and folded into institutional practice.

Physical Plausibility Analysis

Major Freeman effected a felony arrest without inspecting the alleged scene or testing any physical premise of the accuser’s account against objective data. The account requires mutually incompatible conditions to coexist simultaneously — conditions the investigation never attempted to resolve.

Lunar & Weather Conditions

At 2:51 a.m. on October 19, 2019, the moon was approximately 75% illuminated (public lunar data). Temperature was 43°F at 93% humidity (NOAA records). The accuser claimed “complete darkness” and that conditions “felt good.” Neither claim is consistent with the objective record. Freeman obtained neither data set before arrest.

Terrain vs. Claimed Escape Route

The direction the accuser described fleeing leads into an open, flat airfield — not woods and briars. To reach Route 1 as claimed she would have had to reverse direction and run back toward the house. Freeman never walked the route, photographed it, or asked the accuser to explain the geographic impossibility.

Witness Condition at the Bar

Steve Happel’s uncontradicted trial testimony: “that girl kind of came out of nowhere and started being around us and acting kind of hysterical… she gave someone her keys ‘cuz she was too drunk…” This was never reconciled with the prosecution’s account of that evening.

Irreconcilable Physics

The testimony simultaneously requires: complete darkness, concealment in woods and briars, clear identification of a specific vehicle type, identification of the specific driver, mutual eye contact, and the driver waving from the highway. If darkness and concealment hold, identification collapses. If identification holds, darkness and concealment evaporate. These are not minor discrepancies — they are the central mechanics of the account.

Physiological Constraints Never Evaluated

The accuser’s reported height, weight, intoxication level, and footwear were never evaluated against the claimed ability to outrun two trained Army soldiers across rough terrain. Bed height (~36 in.), one-hand wrist restraint mechanics, and simultaneous removal of tight clothing were never tested against basic biomechanics.

What Was Never Done

Photographs were taken at the scene — and that is where the investigation stopped. No measurements. No sketches. No route walk-through. No lunar or weather data obtained. No biomechanical assessment. The destruction-of-evidence insinuation was advanced without any baseline assessment of what evidence had ever existed at the scene.
