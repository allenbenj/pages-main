Seven Factors

State v. Allen presents seven factors that are recognizable patterns in the wrongful-conviction research literature. This case is not a unique outlier and not a borderline evidentiary dispute.

  • Complainant with institutional access to law enforcement
  • Investigator with a personal relationship to the complainant's circle
  • Suppression or re-framing of exculpatory material at the investigation stage
  • Prosecutorial charging decision treating investigative momentum as a substitute for independent evidence review
  • Video evidence contradicting the narrative
  • Physical impossibility in the complainant's narrative
  • Uninvestigated financial motive

The information on this website provides the population-level context for understanding why a case with this profile exists and how it survives. It does not require special cynicism or conspiracy thinking. It requires only that the documented facts be held against the documented research, and the result observed.

Guiding Principle

True corruption arises when a decision-maker understands that a person may be innocent—or that the evidence is insufficient—and still seeks or accepts a conviction. That is not mere error; it is a conscious abandonment of legal and ethical obligations. A system reaches the same corrupt condition in effect when it tolerates or incentivizes outcomes where conviction outweighs proof—whether through pressure, tunnel vision, or procedural shortcuts. Even where individuals claim good faith, the integrity of the system degrades the moment its safeguards—proof beyond a reasonable doubt, full disclosure, and impartial adjudication—cease to operate as real constraints rather than formalities.
